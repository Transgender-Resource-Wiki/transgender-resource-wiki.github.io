# Does the Genderbread Person exclude genderfluid people?

<p style="font-size:1.3em;">
I got an email pointing out to me that <a href="https://www.itspronouncedmetrosexual.com/genderbread-person/#community">my versions of the Genderbread Person</a> exclude genderfluid people. And not just people who use the term “genderfluid” to identify themselves, but anyone who expresses gender differently at different times. So, do they? Let’s figure it out.
</p>
<p>
First, I want to say what I pretty much always say when I get asked a question about <a href="https://www.itspronouncedmetrosexual.com/tags/genderbread/">Genderbread</a>: it’s not perfect, and it’s a forever work in progress. That’s why I’ve released so many different versions, all based on wonderful insight and pointed constructive criticism like this question. I addressed this in my <a href="https://www.itspronouncedmetrosexual.com/videos/gender-from-checkboxes-to-continuums/">Nerd Nite Talk “Gender: From Boxes to -Ness."</a>
</p>
<p>
So nothing that follows is my trying to say this model can’t be improved, or doesn’t have issues. It can and it does. That’s what we’re doing here!
</p>
<p>
Okay, so wrapped cozily in that weighted blanket caveat, <b>does the Genderbread Person exclude genderfluid people? Or not make room for gender fluidity in general?</b>
</p>
<p>
I don’t think so.
</p>
<p>
Here’s why:
</p>
<p>
The Genderbread Person is a tool for helping ourselves and others understand gender – both our own, others’, and gender in general. Like any tool, it’s efficacy is really dependent upon how well you use it.
</p>
<p><i>
Just because you’ve only used a hammer for building, doesn’t mean it’s not great at dismantling. That’s what those little claws on the back are for.
</i></p>
<p>
Now, of course, the Genderbread Person, like any other tool, has its limitations.
</p>
<p>
For example, the Genderbread Person <b>is not</b> a great tool for depicting comprehensive sexuality (that’s why we have the <a href="https://www.itspronouncedmetrosexual.com/2016/05/sexualitree-model-of-comprehensive-sexuality/">Sexualitree</a>).
</p>
<p><i>
Try as you might, a hammer is never good for screwing. That’s why we have screwdrivers.
</i></p>
<p>
However, <b>it is</b> well-suited to include and depict genderfuid people and gender fluidity, if you use it right.
</p>
<p>
<b>The problem, in this case, isn’t the tool itself, but the ways a lot of people might be using it.</b> And what is the “right” way to use the tool in this case?
</p>
<p>
Well, you just have to have two things in mind if you’re using Genderbread to describe yourself, or include bits of instructions when you’re using it with others:
</p>
<h1>
1. You’re not limited to a single dot.
</h1>
<p><b>
You are invited to see yourself as existing anywhere along any of the continua (or scales). Including (and especially!) in multiple places!
</b></p>
<p>
For example, on the gender identity scales, you might see yourself as existing <i>between</i> the roughly 20% point and 60% point of “woman-ness” <b>and</b> 40% point and 80% point on the “man-ness.”
</p>
